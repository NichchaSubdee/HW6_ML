Ethical Considerations of Including Race in a Disease Prediction Model

  1. In this analysis, we’ve used Race as one of the predictors in the model. Briefly discuss the ethical considerations of including race in a disease prediction model. (no more than 1-2 paragraphs)

Answer: Including Race1 as a predictor in a diabetes prediction model raises important ethical concerns. Race is not a biological variable — it is a social and political construct that reflects historical patterns of discrimination, structural inequities in healthcare access, food environments, and chronic stress. When a model learns associations between race and diabetes, it risks encoding and perpetuating systemic health disparities rather than identifying modifiable risk factors. This may lead to racialized predictions that obscure the true social determinants driving health inequities, and may disadvantage already marginalized groups in clinical or policy applications.

A second concern involves the potential for harm in deployment. If a race-inclusive model is used to guide clinical resource allocation or screening decisions, it could lead to discriminatory outcomes — either over-screening certain racial groups (reinforcing stigma) or under-screening others (causing missed diagnoses). Best practices in health data science recommend critically evaluating whether race adds predictive value beyond socioeconomic variables such as HHIncome and Education, which may more directly capture the structural drivers of diabetes risk. If race does not meaningfully improve model performance after accounting for these variables, it should be excluded to reduce the risk of perpetuating bias.
